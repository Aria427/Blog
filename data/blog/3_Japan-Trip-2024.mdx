After falling in love with Japan in 2023, we knew we had to return. But this time, we wanted something different - less of the well-trodden tourist paths, more of the Japan that existed beyond Tokyo and Kyoto. So in May 2024, we headed north for over a month of exploring Tohoku and Hokkaido.

Six weeks. Thirteen different bases. A complete adventure through Japan's quieter, wilder regions.

## The Journey Begins: Back to Tokyo

The journey started familiar - early morning at Malta airport, layover in Istanbul, then the long flight to Tokyo. Landing at Haneda on May 9th felt like coming home. After sorting IC cards and exchanging our JR passes, we made our way to Forest Hongo by Unito in Bunkyo Ward, a quieter neighbourhood than Asakusa where we'd stayed before.

Tokyo was our soft landing back into Japan - three days to shake off jet lag, explore the neighbourhood, and ease into the trip ahead.

## Tea, Tradition, and Kendo

Our first full day included something we'd been looking forward to for months - a traditional tea ceremony at 庵an東京 in Chiyoda. The 90-minute session wasn't just about drinking matcha (though that was delicious). We learned to make nerikiri and ohigashi sweets, each movement deliberate and meaningful. The instructor's patience as we fumbled through the steps, the quiet room, the precise rituals - it felt like stepping into a different pace of life entirely.

The next day took us to Kawagoe, the "Little Edo" north of Tokyo with its preserved merchant houses and bell tower. Then we headed to Shibuya for something completely different - Bushizo Shop for kendo equipment. Spending hours in a specialty martial arts store in a Shibuya high-rise, trying bamboo swords and protective gear, showed us yet again how Tokyo can shift from traditional to modern in minutes.

## Kamakura's Coastal Temples

Sunday was for Kamakura and Enoshima Island. The coastal train ride down was gorgeous, and Kamakura delivered everything we'd hoped for - the Great Buddha sitting peacefully outdoors, temples tucked into hillsides, and narrow streets leading to shrines. Enoshima Island added beaches, caves, and ocean views. We climbed to the observation tower, explored the shrine grounds, and watched the sunset over the Pacific.

After years of wanting to visit Kamakura, it didn't disappoint.

## Kusatsu Onsen: Hot Springs and Mountain Air

On May 13th, we left Tokyo and headed to Kusatsu Onsen in Gunma. The journey into the mountains felt like entering a different world - thick forests, winding roads, cooler air. Checking into Hotel Spax Kusatsu, we could smell the sulfur from the hot springs before we even unpacked.

Kusatsu is one of Japan's most famous onsen towns, and it lives up to the reputation. The Yubatake (hot water field) sits in the centre of town, steam rising constantly from the mineral-rich waters. We spent two days doing very little - soaking in various onsen baths, wandering the small town, eating hearty mountain food, and feeling our shoulders drop from weeks of tension.

This was our first proper onsen town experience in Japan, and it taught us the value of slowing down.

## Nagano and Matsumoto Castle

From the mountains of Kusatsu, we travelled to Nagano city. We'd visited briefly in 2023 for the snow monkeys, but this time we stayed longer and used it as a base to explore. Zenkoji Temple was just as impressive as we remembered - one of Japan's oldest Buddhist temples with pilgrims and tourists mixing in the incense smoke.

The day trip to Matsumoto was a highlight. Matsumoto Castle, one of Japan's original castles with its black exterior and alpine backdrop, was stunning. We explored the steep wooden stairs inside, checked out the city's old merchant streets, and grabbed soba noodles at a local spot. The castle against the mountains is an image that's stayed with us.

## Into Tohoku: Sendai

May 17th marked a shift - we left Chubu and headed north into Tohoku. Sendai, the largest city in the region, became our base for four nights at Henn na Hotel Sendai Kokubuncho. Sendai felt different from Tokyo - still urban and modern, but more relaxed, less overwhelming.

We explored the city's arcades, visited Zuihoden (Date Masamune's mausoleum), and discovered gyutan (grilled beef tongue), a Sendai specialty that we became mildly obsessed with.

## Matsushima's Island Views

Matsushima Bay, just a short train ride from Sendai, is considered one of Japan's three most scenic views - and we can see why. Hundreds of small pine-covered islands dot the bay. We took a sightseeing boat between them, visited Zuiganji Temple, and walked across the red bridge to Godaido Hall.

The islands, the temples, the coastal walk - Matsushima had a quiet beauty that felt worlds away from Sendai's city energy.

## Zao Fox Village: Furry Chaos

Then came one of the strangest, most delightful experiences of the entire trip - Zao Fox Village. A sanctuary in the mountains where foxes roam semi-freely, and you can walk among them.

It was chaotic. Foxes everywhere - sleeping, playing, begging for food, staring at visitors with those intense eyes. Some were shy, others bold enough to approach. The fluffier winter foxes were still shedding their coats. We spent hours just watching them interact, nap in the sun, and occasionally cause mischief.

If you love animals and don't mind a bit of fox-related chaos, this place is magic.

## Moving North: Morioka

We moved to Morioka in Iwate Prefecture on May 21st, checking into Hotel Metropolitan Morioka New Wing. Morioka became one of our favourite bases - a smaller city with great food (wanko soba, reimen, morioka cold noodles), friendly locals, and easy access to day trips.

## Hiraizumi and Tono: History and Folklore

Hiraizumi, a short train ride away, is a UNESCO World Heritage site. Chuson-ji Temple with its Golden Hall (Konjikido) was breathtaking - gold leaf covering the interior, centuries of history in one small building. The temple grounds, gardens, and peaceful hillside made for a perfect half-day.

Tono was different - a town known for folklore and traditional Japanese countryside life. We visited Tono Furusato Village to see traditional magariya farmhouses and learn about local legends. The slower pace and rural landscapes felt restorative.

## Akita: Kakunodate and Ancient Forests

Moving west to Akita on May 24th, we checked into Comfort Hotel Akita and spent three days exploring this coastal prefecture. Kakunodate, the "Little Kyoto of Tohoku," had beautiful samurai district streets lined with cherry trees (though we missed the blossoms). The preserved samurai houses and quiet atmosphere were lovely.

Then came Shirakami Sanchi - a UNESCO World Heritage beech forest straddling Akita and Aomori. Hiking through ancient trees, some over 400 years old, with dappled sunlight filtering through the canopy, felt primordial. The air was different there - clean, cool, almost sacred.

## Aomori: Apple Country and Ancient Settlements

Aomori became our longest Tohoku base - five nights at Hotel Aomori starting May 27th. Aomori city felt frontier-like, perched at Honshu's northern tip. We explored the waterfront, visited the impressive Nebuta Museum WARASSE showcasing the region's festival floats, and ate more seafood than we thought possible.

San'nai-Maruyama, a Jomon period archaeological site, was fascinating - reconstructed pit dwellings and longhouses from over 5,000 years ago. Standing in those ancient structures made Japanese history feel suddenly tangible.

We also took a half-day trip to Hirosaki, famous for its castle and cherry blossoms (we were too late for the blossoms, but the castle and park were still beautiful). Hirosaki felt more intimate than Aomori, with great coffee shops and a slower vibe.

## Crossing to Hokkaido: Hakodate

On May 31st, we crossed from Honshu to Hokkaido, checking into Hotel & Spa Century Marina Hakodate. Hakodate felt like the gateway to Japan's northern island - still connected to Tohoku's energy but with Hokkaido's frontier spirit.

We spent three days exploring. Mount Hakodate's night view is famous (one of Japan's top three night views), and standing up there watching the city lights spread between two harbours was spectacular. The historic Motomachi district with its Western-style buildings showed Hakodate's history as an early international port. And Hakodate Morning Market - wandering through stalls of fresh seafood, trying ikura don and grilled scallops for breakfast - became a daily ritual.

## Sapporo: Northern Capital

From Hakodate, we travelled to Sapporo - Hokkaido's capital and largest city. Checking into PINN-N4E2I on June 3rd, we stayed for five nights.

Sapporo felt vibrant. We wandered through Odori Park, visited the Clock Tower and Former Hokkaido Government Office, explored Susukino's nightlife district, and ate at ramen alley (Sapporo's miso ramen is something special). The city had energy but felt more spacious than Tokyo, with wider streets and mountain views.

One afternoon, we took a half-day trip to Otaru, a canal town west of Sapporo. The old warehouses converted into shops and cafes, the glass-blowing workshops, the fresh seafood - Otaru had charm without feeling overly touristy.

## Asahikawa and Daisetsuzan

Moving to Asahikawa on June 8th marked our furthest north. Smaller than Sapporo but still substantial, Asahikawa felt relaxed. We stayed at Premier Hotel Cabin Asahikawa and made a day trip to Daisetsuzan National Park - Hokkaido's largest national park.

The park was wild in the best way. We hiked through volcanic landscapes, saw fumaroles venting steam, and walked trails through alpine meadows just starting to bloom. The scale of Hokkaido's wilderness hit us there - this was nature on a different level than we'd seen elsewhere in Japan.

## Noboribetsu: Hot Spring Haven

From Asahikawa, we returned south to Noboribetsu, one of Hokkaido's premier onsen towns. Checking into Noboribetsu Sekisui Tei on June 11th felt luxurious after weeks of moving around. This ryokan had multiple hot spring baths, traditional kaiseki dinners, and that particular kind of Japanese hospitality that makes you feel completely cared for.

We explored Jigokudani (Hell Valley), a volcanic crater with steam vents and sulfurous streams creating an otherworldly landscape. Walking the trails through this active geothermal area, with steam rising everywhere and the smell of sulfur in the air, was both beautiful and slightly unnerving.

Noboribetsu was where we truly understood Hokkaido's volcanic nature - the island sits on a ring of fire, and you can feel it beneath your feet.

## The Ferry Journey: Tomakomai to Oarai

After a brief night in Tomakomai, we embarked on one of the trip's most unique experiences - an overnight ferry from Tomakomai to Oarai. Boarding MOL Sunflower Sapporo on June 14th at 6 PM, we settled into our cabin as Hokkaido's coastline slowly disappeared.

The 19-hour ferry journey was meditative. We had a private cabin, access to observation decks, onboard restaurants, and even a bath. Watching the Pacific Ocean from the deck, sleeping to the gentle rocking of the ship, waking to see Honshu's coast approaching - it felt like a proper transition between islands.

Landing in Oarai on June 15th, we'd completed a huge loop through northern Japan.

## Full Circle: Back to Tokyo

The trip ended where it began - Tokyo. But this time we stayed in Edogawa Ward at Henn na Hotel Tokyo Nishikasai, a different part of the city. With five days left, we packed in the highlights we'd missed: Tokyo Disneysea (pure entertainment and impressive theming), a day trip to Nikko with its elaborate shrines and natural beauty, and general Tokyo wandering.

By this point, six weeks in, we moved through Tokyo differently than we had in 2023. The train system felt intuitive, ordering food was automatic, and the city's rhythm made sense. We'd become comfortable.

## Reflections: Northern Japan

Flying out of Haneda on June 20th, exhausted but satisfied, we realized this trip had been completely different from 2023. That first trip was about Japan's icons - Tokyo, Kyoto, the famous sights. This trip was about regions most international visitors skip.

Tohoku and Hokkaido showed us a quieter, wilder, more rugged Japan. Smaller cities with fierce regional pride, ancient forests, active volcanoes, folklore alive in rural towns, and onsen culture at its finest. Spending extended time in each place - we weren't just visiting these regions, we were living in them, briefly becoming part of their daily rhythm.

The trip taught us that Japan's depth is endless. You can return again and again and still discover something new. Northern Japan, with its dramatic landscapes and understated charm, has secured a permanent place in our travel memories.

And honestly? We're already thinking about when we can go back.
