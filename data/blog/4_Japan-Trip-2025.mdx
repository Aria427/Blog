By 2025, Japan had become a regular destination - almost a second home. After exploring central Honshu in 2023 and northern Japan in 2024, I decided it was time to properly explore the south. Five weeks in summer to experience Kyoto's famous Gion Matsuri, discover Shikoku's islands, dive into Kyushu's history and hot springs, and finally visit Ghibli Park.

This trip felt different from the start - more relaxed, more confident, more focused on festivals and experiences than ticking off landmarks.

## Back to Kyoto: Gion Matsuri Season

The journey began familiar - Malta to Istanbul to Osaka. Landing at Kansai International Airport on July 14th, I took the JR Haruka train to Kyoto Station and walked to Hotel Tomiya. This was my third time in Kyoto, and checking in felt like returning to a favourite city rather than visiting somewhere new.

But the timing was deliberate. I'd arrived during Gion Matsuri - Kyoto's month-long festival and one of Japan's most famous celebrations. The festival dates back over a thousand years, and being there for it had been on my list since my first trip.

## Himeji Castle and Festival Nights

Before diving into the festival, I took a day trip to Himeji and Akashi. Himeji Castle - Japan's most spectacular surviving castle - was absolutely worth the train ride. The white exterior (earning it the nickname "White Heron Castle"), the defensive architecture, the views from the top - everything about it was impressive. Nearby Akashi added coastal views and a more relaxed pace.

That evening, I returned to Kyoto for the Gion Matsuri yoiyama - the nights before the main parade when streets fill with food stalls, lanterns, and festival energy. Walking through Kyoto's narrow streets packed with people in yukata, trying festival foods, hearing traditional music spilling from the yamaboko floats - the atmosphere was incredible.

## Gion Matsuri: The Main Event

July 17th was the Yamaboko Junko - the main parade. I found a spot along the route early and watched as enormous floats, some weighing over 10 tons and covered in intricate tapestries, were pulled through Kyoto's streets. The coordination required to turn these massive wooden structures around corners using only ropes and manpower was mesmerizing.

The parade lasted hours. Traditional music, historical costumes, centuries-old floats - it felt like Kyoto's history moving through its present-day streets. This was exactly the kind of cultural experience I'd hoped for.

## Osaka Expo 2025

The next day brought something completely different - Osaka Expo 2025. After taking the train from Kyoto to Yumeshima, I spent the day exploring pavilions showcasing future technologies, sustainable innovations, and cultural exhibits from around the world. The contrast between Gion Matsuri's thousand-year traditions and the Expo's forward-looking vision captured something essential about Japan.

The pavilions were hit or miss, but the overall experience was fascinating. Plus, the Expo-exclusive food and merchandise satisfied my collector tendencies.

## Shikoku: Takamatsu and Island Life

On July 19th, I left Kyoto and took a highway bus to Takamatsu on Shikoku island. Checking into Aoisora Aoiumi no guest house marked a shift from hotels to a more intimate accommodation - a guesthouse with shared spaces and opportunities to meet other travelers.

Takamatsu became my base for a week of island exploration. The city itself was pleasant - smaller than Kyoto, with a relaxed coastal vibe. But the real draw was the islands dotting the Seto Inland Sea.

## Shodoshima: Olives and Art

My first island trip was to Shodoshima, the second-largest island in the Seto Inland Sea. Known for olive cultivation (Japan's olive center), Mediterranean-style landscapes, and art installations, Shodoshima felt different from anywhere else I'd been in Japan.

I visited the Angel Road - a sandbar that appears at low tide connecting small islands. We explored olive groves, saw the "Olive Tree in Love" (two olive trees whose branches have grown together), and checked out art pieces left from the Setouchi Triennale. The island had a dreamy, creative energy.

## Island Hopping: Ogijima and Megijima

The smaller islands offered even more concentrated experiences. Ogijima, with its steep streets, cat population, and art scattered throughout the village, felt like a secret discovery. The island is tiny - you can walk around it in a couple hours - but every corner revealed something interesting: a sculpture, a painted wall, a hidden viewpoint.

Megijima was similar but with its own character - cave systems, more forest trails, and fewer visitors. These islands showed me a side of Japan that exists far from the tourist circuits: small communities, aging populations, art projects breathing new life into traditional spaces.

## Shikoku Mura and Kotohira

Back on Shikoku's main island, I visited Shikoku Mura - an open-air museum of relocated traditional buildings. Walking through farmhouses, bridges, and village structures from across Shikoku gave me a sense of the region's architectural history.

The day trip to Kotohira to visit Konpira-san (Kotohira-gu shrine) involved climbing 1,368 stone steps up the mountainside. The climb was tough in July heat, but the shrine complex, views, and sense of accomplishment made it worthwhile. Plus, seeing elderly locals making the climb put my complaining into perspective.

## Fukuoka: Gateway to Kyushu

Leaving Takamatsu on July 27th, I took trains through Okayama to Hakata Station in Fukuoka. For the next ten days, Fukuoka became my Kyushu base. The apartment I'd rented was in a local neighborhood - a proper glimpse into daily life rather than tourist Fukuoka.

Fukuoka felt energetic and modern but manageable. Tenjin's shopping districts, the yatai (food stalls) along the river at night, the mix of beach and city - Fukuoka had its own rhythm. I quickly became attached to the local convenience store breakfasts and evening walks through the neighborhood.

## Dazaifu and Nanzoin Temple

Just outside Fukuoka, Dazaifu offered a historical contrast. The Dazaifu Tenmangu shrine, dedicated to the deity of learning, had beautiful grounds and the usual student visitors praying for exam success. The nearby Kyushu National Museum provided context for the region's history as Japan's gateway to mainland Asia.

Nanzoin Temple delivered one of the trip's most surprising moments - a 41-meter bronze reclining Buddha, one of the largest in the world. The statue's sheer scale was remarkable, but the temple grounds with thousands of smaller stone statues were equally captivating.

## Nagasaki: History and Reflection

The day trip to Nagasaki was emotionally heavy but important. Visiting the Atomic Bomb Museum and Peace Park, seeing the reconstructed Urakami Cathedral, and learning about the city's history created a profound experience. Nagasaki's message of peace and the city's resilience were powerful.

Beyond the historical sites, Nagasaki's hilly streets, Chinatown, and harbor showed a city shaped by international influences. The contrast between the heavy history and the vibrant, forward-looking city was striking.

## Hiroshima: Remembering and Moving Forward

Similarly, the day trip to Hiroshima combined sobering historical education with appreciation for a rebuilt city. The Peace Memorial Park, the A-Bomb Dome preserved as a UNESCO site, and the museum's detailed exhibits made the reality of August 6, 1945 tangible.

But Hiroshima is also a thriving modern city. Wandering through the rebuilt downtown, seeing the trams, eating Hiroshima-style okonomiyaki - the city demonstrates recovery and resilience. Both Nagasaki and Hiroshima reminded me that travel can be about learning and reflection, not just enjoyment.

## Meeting Ito and Yanagawa

Back in Fukuoka, I met up with Ito, a friend I'd stayed in touch with since my first trip. Having a local show me their favorite spots - the best ramen place, a hidden shrine, the view from a neighborhood park - added depth to my Fukuoka experience.

We took a day trip to Yanagawa, a canal town south of Fukuoka. Riding a donkobune (traditional boat) through narrow waterways past old buildings and under low bridges felt like floating through old Japan. The boat guide sang traditional songs and pointed out historical details. It was charming and peaceful.

## Beppu: Hot Spring Heaven

Leaving Fukuoka on August 6th, I took a highway bus to Beppu - one of Japan's most famous onsen towns. Checking into Seaside Hotel Mimatsu Ooetei, a traditional ryokan, I settled in for three days of doing essentially nothing but soaking in hot springs.

Beppu is unique because of its "hells" - spectacular hot springs too hot for bathing, each with different colors and characteristics from the minerals and geothermal activity. I toured the hells: the cobalt blue Umi Jigoku, the blood-red Chinoike Jigoku, the steaming Kamado Jigoku. They were otherworldly.

But the real draw was the baths. The ryokan had multiple onsen fed by different natural springs. I spent hours rotating between them, reading, napping, eating kaiseki dinners, and fully embracing the onsen lifestyle. After weeks of moving around, Beppu was exactly the break I needed.

## Return to Kyoto: Final Explorations

Leaving Beppu on August 9th, I took trains through Kokura back to Kyoto. Returning to Hotel Tomiya felt like closing a loop - I'd started here, traveled through Shikoku and Kyushu, and now had a final week to explore the Kansai region properly.

## Death Stranding and Pop Culture

One of the stranger (but most fun) days was visiting the Death Stranding Popup Tour at Shinsaibashi Parco in Osaka. As a Kojima Productions fan, seeing props, costumes, and art from Death Stranding 2 before the game's release was exciting. The popup had photo opportunities, exclusive merchandise, and other fans equally excited about the upcoming game.

This mix of traditional temples and modern pop culture experiences is peak Japan for me - both exist simultaneously without contradiction.

## Nara Revisited

I'd visited Nara in 2023, but returning felt different with more experience and less pressure to see everything. I spent the day in Nara Park with the deer, revisited Todaiji, explored some smaller temples I'd missed, and enjoyed wandering without an agenda. Sometimes the best travel days are the unstructured ones.

## Ghibli Park: A Dream Visit

August 13th was one of my most anticipated days - Ghibli Park in Aichi. I'd secured tickets months in advance for Ghibli's Grand Warehouse, the main area featuring sets and scenes from Studio Ghibli films.

The experience exceeded expectations. Walking through life-size recreations of iconic scenes - the bathhouse from Spirited Away, Baron's room from Whisper of the Heart, the Catbus, detailed exhibits on animation production - felt like stepping into Miyazaki's worlds. The attention to detail was remarkable.

As someone who grew up with Ghibli films, this was pure joy. I spent hours exploring every corner, taking photos, and getting emotional over childhood memories brought to life.

## Uji, Lake Biwa, and Local Kyoto

The final days were gentler explorations. Uji, famous for matcha tea, offered temples, the Tale of Genji Museum, and excellent green tea everything. Lake Biwa, Japan's largest freshwater lake, provided nature and peaceful lakeside walks in Otsu.

Even Yamashina, technically part of Kyoto but often overlooked, had its own temples and neighborhood charm. These low-key day trips showed me that even after multiple visits, there's always more to discover in the Kansai region.

## Reflections: The Third Time Around

Flying out of Kansai Airport on August 17th, I realized this trip had been about depth over breadth. Instead of cramming in as many famous sights as possible, I'd focused on experiences: a major festival, island life, historical reflection, onsen culture, pop culture celebrations, and simply being in places long enough to develop routines.

Three trips to Japan, three completely different experiences. The first was about major sights and first impressions. The second explored lesser-known northern regions. This third trip balanced both - returning to Kyoto with more understanding while discovering new regions in Shikoku and Kyushu.

Southern Japan showed me a different side of the country: island communities keeping traditions alive through art, Kyushu's complex history, festival culture at its peak, and how Japan continues balancing tradition with contemporary creativity. Each visit teaches me that Japan's depth is inexhaustible.

And yes, I'm already planning the fourth trip. Maybe Okinawa next time?
