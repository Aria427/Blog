After three trips to Japan, we were ready to explore somewhere new in East Asia. South Korea had been on my radar for a while - the food, the culture, the mix of ultra-modern cities and traditional villages. This trip would take us through Seoul, Jeju Island, Busan and eventually across to Okinawa, but this first part covers the Korean leg of the journey.

## Getting There: Malta to Seoul

The journey started on a Friday evening - a 19:25 flight from Malta to Istanbul, landing at 22:55. After a nearly three-hour layover at Istanbul Airport, we boarded the 01:50 flight to Seoul. Nine hours and fifty minutes later, we touched down at Incheon International Airport at 17:40 local time on Saturday.

First order of business at Incheon was picking up T-Money cards - South Korea's public transport card, the equivalent of Japan's IC cards. We bought bus tickets and took the 6002 Airport Limousine bus toward Jongno. The ride took about an hour and a half, followed by an eight-minute walk to our accommodation. Checking in at the Tradiup Hotel by the Designers in Jongno, we had our first base sorted. Seoul was ready.

## Seoul Day One: Palaces and Hanok Villages

Sunday was our first full day, and we dove straight into Seoul's historic heart. We started at Ikseon-dong Hanok Village - a neighbourhood of traditional Korean houses (hanok) that have been converted into cafes, boutiques, and restaurants. The narrow alleyways had a charm that felt intimate compared to the wider streets of modern Seoul. It was a perfect introduction to how Korea preserves its traditional architecture while giving it new life.

From there, we walked to Changdeokgung, one of Seoul's Five Grand Palaces and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The palace complex was beautiful - elaborate gates, painted wooden buildings, and the famous Secret Garden behind the main halls. Moving on to Bukchon Hanok Village, we wandered the sloping streets lined with hundreds of traditional hanok houses, many still residential. The views down through the tiled rooftops toward the modern city skyline captured Seoul's contrast between old and new perfectly.

The afternoon brought Gyeongbokgung Palace, the largest and most iconic of the Five Grand Palaces. The scale was impressive - wide courtyards, the throne hall, the pavilion overlooking the reflecting pond. Everywhere, visitors in rented hanbok (traditional Korean clothing) added splashes of colour against the ancient architecture.

We ended the day with a walk along Cheongyecheon, a restored stream running through central Seoul. The waterway, lined with walking paths and lit up at night, was a calming contrast to the palace grandeur. A quick stop at Ssamzigil, a creative shopping complex in Insadong with its spiralling walkways and artisan shops, rounded off a packed first day.

## Namsan Mountain and Myeongdong

Monday was all about views and street life. We took the cable car up Namsan Mountain to Seoul Tower, one of the city's most recognisable landmarks. The panoramic views from the top stretched across the entire Seoul basin - endless high-rises fading into the surrounding mountains. The mountain itself was a pleasant escape, with walking trails through the forest just minutes from the city centre.

Coming down, we headed straight to Myeongdong. The shopping street was buzzing with energy - skincare shops, fashion stores, and street food stalls competing for attention. Myeongdong Market was where we really got into the food scene: tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes), hotteok (sweet filled pancakes), and skewered everything. The density of it all - the noise, the smells, the neon signs - reminded us of Dotonbori in Osaka, but with a distinctly Korean energy.

## Nami Island Day Trip

Tuesday was dedicated to a day trip outside Seoul. We caught the 09:10 shuttle bus from Dongdaemun History & Culture Park Station (Exit 9) to Nami Island. The half-moon shaped island in the Han River is famous for its tree-lined paths and as a filming location for the K-drama Winter Sonata.

Getting to the island was an experience in itself - we took the zip line across rather than the ferry. Soaring over the water to the island below was exhilarating and set the tone for the day. On the island, we wandered along paths canopied by towering trees - metasequoias, ginkgos, pines - each avenue creating its own atmosphere. The place had a storybook quality to it, peaceful and slightly otherworldly.

The 16:30 shuttle bus brought us back to Dongdaemun by 18:00, leaving the evening free in Seoul.

## Temples, Parks, and Art

Wednesday was a more relaxed exploration of Seoul. We visited Jogyesa Temple, the chief temple of the Jogye Order of Korean Buddhism, right in the middle of the city. The colourful lanterns strung across the courtyard and the ancient trees on the grounds created an oasis of calm among the surrounding office buildings.

Nearby, Tapgol Park offered historical weight - it's the site where Korea's Declaration of Independence was first read publicly in 1919. The park's monuments and reliefs told the story of the March 1st Movement, adding a layer of understanding about Korean history that went beyond palaces and temples.

The afternoon was spent at the National Museum of Modern and Contemporary Art, where we lost track of time moving through exhibitions of Korean and international contemporary art. The museum's architecture itself was worth the visit - a renovated historic building blended with modern additions.

## Moving South: Seoul to Jeju

Thursday marked the end of our time in Seoul. After checking out at noon, we made our way to Gimpo International Airport for the 13:15 flight to Jeju. Just an hour and fifteen minutes later, we landed at Jeju Airport - domestic flights in Korea are impressively quick.
