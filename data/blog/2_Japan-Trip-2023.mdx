I finally made it to Japan in 2023! After years of dreaming about it, stepping off the plane at Haneda felt surreal - like I'd walked into a world I'd only imagined.

Three weeks exploring central Honshu completely changed how I think about travel. It wasn't just a holiday - it was an adventure that stayed with me long after I left.

## Arriving in Tokyo: Jet Lag and First Impressions

The journey started early in Malta - sunrise airport check-in, layover in Frankfurt, then a 13-hour night flight to Tokyo. When we landed at Haneda on April 13th, we were exhausted but buzzing with excitement. After grabbing SIM cards and figuring out the IC card ticket machines, we hopped on the train to Asakusa and watched Tokyo unfold around us. Our hotel, Richmond Hotel Premier Asakusa International, became our peaceful home base - a quiet retreat between temple bells and pachinko parlours.

That first evening was full of little wins - finding the right station exit, not getting lost in the narrow streets, buying snacks at our first konbini. I was surprised by how quiet Tokyo felt, even on busy streets. Everything felt special: the soft chime before train announcements, the way station attendants bowed. It was magical.

## Tsukiji Market and Exploring Tokyo

Jet lag worked in our favour on day one. We were up early and on the train to Tsukiji, watching Tokyo wake up as the sun rose. At Tsukiji Honganji, we met our guides and fellow food lovers for a three-hour tour of one of Tokyo's best markets. The morning was incredible - warm tamagoyaki, glossy tuna slices, sake samples, seared seafood skewers, and finally, my first proper sushi meal in Japan. It was clean, precise, and absolutely delicious.

Tokyo kept surprising us. We wandered through Ginza's fancy boutiques, then visited the Art Aquarium Museum where goldfish swam through illuminated glass sculptures. A walk around the Imperial Palace grounds gave us some green space and calm. By late afternoon, we were at teamLab Planets in Toyosu - barefoot, walking through mirrored rooms and shallow pools where art and light blended together. By the time we got back to Asakusa that night, our feet hurt but our minds were buzzing.

## Temples, Vintage Rides, and Anime Culture

The next day, we explored Asakusa properly. Sensoji's huge red lantern was impressive, and somewhere between the main hall and side shrines, I bought my first goshuincho (temple stamp book). Getting that first calligraphy stamp felt special. The streets around the temple - Nakamise-dori, Shin-Nakamise, and Nishi Sando - were packed with snack stalls and souvenir shops.

Asakusa Hanayashiki, one of Japan's oldest amusement parks, felt like stepping back in time with its creaky rides and pastel colours. Later, we took the train to Akihabara and the vibe completely changed - massive anime billboards, game centres full of claw machines, and multi-floor hobby shops. It was amazing how Tokyo could feel so traditional one minute and so futuristic the next.

## Shibuya, Harajuku, and City Parks

One morning we headed to Shibuya. After stopping at Hachiko's statue, we crossed the famous Shibuya Crossing - pure organised chaos as hundreds of people crossed from all directions. We then walked down Cat Street, a quieter stretch with boutiques and cafes, heading toward Harajuku.

Harajuku delivered exactly what I expected - colour, crowds, and crepes on Takeshita Dori. But just minutes away, Yoyogi Park and Meiji Jingu shrine felt like a different world. Walking through the gravel paths and towering trees, with birdsong replacing city noise, showed me how Tokyo balances tradition and modernity so well.

## Nagano's Snow Monkeys

On the 17th, we left Tokyo and took the Hokuriku Shinkansen north to Nagano. Watching the city fade into countryside from the train was beautiful. In Nagano, we grabbed a Snow Monkey 1-Day Pass and took a bus into the hills to Jigokudani Monkey Park.

The walk to the park was a gentle forest trail before we reached the hot spring - full of Japanese macaques just chilling in the onsen water. They barely noticed us, grooming each other while we took photos. It was surreal and peaceful. Back in Nagano city, we visited Zenkoji Temple before heading back to Tokyo.

## Old Tokyo Neighbourhoods and Ueno

Another day we explored Yanesen, one of Tokyo's atmospheric old neighbourhoods. We walked through Yanaka Cemetery where cherry blossoms were falling, then checked out Yanaka Ginza with its retro shops and snack stalls - it felt like old Tokyo. Nezu Shrine's torii gates and mossy grounds were beautiful.

In Ueno, the energy picked up. We visited Ueno Zoo, walked through Ueno Park, and explored the busy Ameyoko market with its street food and market stalls.

## Hakone: Hot Springs and Mountain Views

On the 20th, we left Tokyo for Hakone. Getting there was an adventure - train to Tokyo Station, grabbed an ekiben lunch box, Shinkansen to Odawara, then used our Hakone Freepass to connect buses and trains. By the time we reached Lake Ashi and checked into Hotel Musashiya, the mountain air felt amazing.

Hakone was where we slowed down. We visited Amazake-chaya, a traditional teahouse on the Old Tokaido Road, warming up with sweet amazake and mochi. Evenings were for kaiseki dinners at the ryokan and long soaks in the onsen overlooking the lake. This was when I really understood Japanese hospitality.

## Ropeways, Black Eggs, and Outdoor Art

We spent days exploring Hakone. A cruise on Lake Ashi took us past the red torii gate rising from the water. From Tōgendai, we took the Hakone Ropeway up to Owakudani where volcanic steam and dark rock created an otherworldly landscape. We ate black eggs boiled in the hot springs (they're supposed to add years to your life!).

Later, we visited the Hakone Open Air Museum - sculptures scattered across the landscape with mountain views. Hakone Gora Park added some European-style gardens to the mix. We also visited Hakone Shrine with its lakeside torii and wandered through Hakone Kuzuryu Forest.

## First Days in Kyoto

Leaving Hakone on the 23rd, we took the Shinkansen to Kyoto and checked into Kyoto Gion Hotel. By late afternoon, we were on Hanamikoji Street, soaking in the wooden buildings and lanterns, hoping to spot a geiko or maiko.

Yasaka Shrine and Maruyama Park at night were gorgeous - lanterns flickering everywhere. Kyoto felt quieter and more intimate than Tokyo but just as alive. Those first hours in Gion made me fall for the city immediately.

## Arashiyama Bamboo and Fushimi Inari

The Arashiyama day was stunning. We started at the Monkey Park with city views, crossed Togetsukyo Bridge, then walked into the famous bamboo forest. The towering bamboo stalks created this green tunnel that felt otherworldly.

Tenryuji Temple's zen gardens were peaceful, and the Kimono Forest near the station - illuminated pillars wrapped in kimono fabric - was whimsical. From there, we headed to Fushimi Inari Taisha and climbed through thousands of red torii gates. As we went higher, the crowds thinned out. It was one of those "this is really happening" moments.

## Nara's Deer and Historic Temples

Nara was a day trip from Kyoto. We met up with friends Chihiro and Yuka who showed us around. Isui-en Garden had beautiful ponds and traditional structures. Then we hit Nara Park where the deer approached us, bowing for senbei crackers - so cute!

We walked through Yumekaze Plaza to the massive Nandaimon Gate, then into Todaiji to see the Great Buddha. From there, we visited Kasuga Taisha Shrine and wandered through Naramachi's streets. Having locals show us around made the experience so much better.

## Universal Studios Japan

After all those temples and gardens, we gave ourselves a fun day. We left early from Gion-Shijo to Universal City Station in Osaka. Universal Studios Japan was pure entertainment - movie soundtracks, roller coasters, themed snacks, the works.

Between rides and themed food, the day felt wonderfully light. It was a perfect break before diving back into Kyoto's cultural sites, and it reminded me that travel can be about play and culture.

## Kyoto Markets and Philosopher's Path

Back in Kyoto, we spent a day wandering neighbourhoods. Nishiki Market ("Kitchen of Kyoto") was packed with food vendors - skewered seafood, pickles, sweets, tea. The colours and smells were amazing.

From there, we explored North Higashiyama - Kyoto City Zoo, Okazaki Park, and the impressive Heian Shrine. Then we visited Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion) with its raked sand gardens. Walking the Philosopher's Path afterward, lined with trees and modest homes, was so peaceful. Nanzen-ji Temple with its grand gates finished the day perfectly.

## Mountain Trails: Kibune and Kurama

One of my favourite days was hiking from Kibune to Kurama. We took the train to Kibuneguchi Station and walked through Kibune Village with its riverside ryokan and lantern-lit paths. We visited Kifune-jinja's torii gate and followed the trail up through the forest to Kurama-dera Temple.

At the top, we had expansive views before hiking down to Kurama Village. This hike was a highlight - spiritual, physical, and scenic all at once. It reminded me that Japan's magic isn't just in the famous spots but in the trails connecting them.

## Imperial Palace and Golden Pavilion

We spent more days exploring northern Kyoto. The Kyoto Imperial Palace grounds had broad paths and traditional architecture. Further north, we visited Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion) shimmering against its pond - absolutely stunning.

These last Kyoto days felt like gentle repetitions - walking, visiting shrines, trying snacks, wandering familiar streets. By the end, Kyoto felt both endlessly rich and somehow like home.

## Osaka: Markets, Neon, and Digital Art

We left Kyoto for Kansai Airport Station, checked into Hotel Nikko Kansai Airport, then headed back to Osaka for some urban energy. Kuromon Ichiba Market was our first stop - seafood, fruits, sweets, and street food everywhere. We walked through retro Shinsekai, then into Dotonbori's neon streets with giant crab billboards and animated signs.

On our last full day, we explored Osaka Castle and rode out to Osaka Aquarium Kaiyukan. That night, we visited TeamLab Botanical Garden in Sumiyoshi where art and nature mixed with light installations among real trees. It felt like a perfect bookend to teamLab Planets in Tokyo.

## Heading Home

The final hours came too quickly. From Kansai-Airport Station, we made a short trip to Izumisano to visit Aozora Fishery Market and Rinku Park. That night, we flew to Munich then back to Malta, and just like that, the trip was over. But mentally, Japan stayed with me - the taste of amazake, quiet shrine moments, pachinko sounds, onsen steam.

As a first-time visitor in 2023, Japan exceeded every expectation. It wasn't just the big sights but the small moments - temple stamps, ekiben on the Shinkansen, a station attendant's nod - that made it special. Leaving felt less like ending a trip and more like pausing a story I'll definitely return to someday.
